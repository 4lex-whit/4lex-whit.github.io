Copyright, Designs, & Patents Act 1988

  1. What is the CDPA?
  2. What breaches the CDPA?

What is the CDPA?

The Copyright, Designs, & Patents Act was introduced in 1988, and protects the intellectual property (defined as "intangible creations of the human intellect") of a person or organisation.

Copyright is automatically applied to original pieces of strongwork, and - depending on the type of work - will only expire 25 to 70 years after the owner's death.

What breaches the CDPA?

Breaches of the Copyright, Designs, & Patents Act are divided into two categories; Primary Breaches and Secondary Breaches. However, if a person or organisation has the relevant permission, it does not breach the act.

If someone believes their copyright has been breached, it is their responsibility to take action under the act.

Primary Breaches

  • Copying an original work
  • Issuing the copy of the original work to the public
  • Renting/lending the copy of the original work to the public
  • Performing, showing, or playing the original work in public
  • Making an adaptation of the original work

Secondary Breaches

  • Importing a copy of original work
  • Possessing or dealing with a copy of the original work
  • Providing means to make copies of the original work
  • Permitting the use of premises for making copies of the original work
  • Provision of props/equipment for a performance of a copy of the original work

Computer Misuse Act 1990

  1. What is the CMA?
  2. What are the primary offences?

What is the CMA?

The Computer Misuse Act legislates the malicious use of computers, and was first introduced in 1990 - but is updated regularly to ensure it remains relevant.

It was first introduced due to two people gaining unauthorised access to a British Telecom's account after "shoulder-surfing" the username and password. They were charged under the Forgery and Counterfeiting Act 1981, but acquitted due to the act not being intended for this use case, and they faced no charges. After this, the Computer Misuse Act 1990 was introduced to prevent this happening in the future, and make the laws clearer.

What are the primary offences?

The CMA has three primary offences, and offenders face different charges depending on which is broken - but all can result in time in prison.

  1. Unauthorised Access to Computer Materials
    Example: a student finding out a teacher's password, and then logging into their account and viewing their files.

  2. Unauthorised Access with Intent to Commit Further Offences
    Example: a student finding out a teacher's password, and then logging into their account with the intent to increase their mark on a test.

  3. Unauthorised Modification of Computer Files
    Example: a student finding out a teacher's password, and then logging into their account and increasing their mark on a test.

What is the DPA?

The Data Protection Act was introduced in 1998 to protect the collection, processing, and storing of data. If these laws are broken, the organisation is referred to the Information Commissioner's Office.

Data Collection

According to the Data Protection Act 1998, organisations have to have a legitimate reason to collect data. For example, a revision website could ask to collect your email address so they can contact you, but not things like your height and weight.

The data is categorised into either Personal or Sensitive data. For example, personal data for a college could be a student's name, address, or phone number, while sensitive data could be your policial party, religion, bank account number, etc.

Data is categorised differently depending on the purpose of the organisation - while a bank account number would be sensitive for a college, it would be personal for a bank. A company must be authorised to store sensitive data on a subject.

Data Processing

Data processing must be done lawfully, transparently, and in a fair way. This means the organisation must be clear on what information is stored about a subject, and what it's used for.

The term fair is potentially problematic, as what an organisation deems fair might not be the same as what the subject does.

Data Storage

The Data Protection Act 1998 states that data must be accessible to the subject. This means that, if requested, the organisation has to provide the subject with all the data they have concerning them.

In addition to this, data must be stored in a timely manner. This means that an organisation can only store data for as long as it is necessary, and it must be deleted when no longer. For example, a college would be required to delete your photograph after you leave, as it's no longer required for them.

What is the GDPR?

The GDPR stands for the General Data Protection Regulation, and it was introduced by the European Union to further regulate the processing of person data in 2018.

This regulation gave subjects more rights about their data, allowing them to request data be deleted, and forcing organisations to request to store data on children.

Regulation of Investigatory Powers Act 2000

What is the RIPA?

The RIPA stands for the Regulation of Investigatory Powers Act 2000, and it legislates how organisations can monitor electronic communications. The term electronic communications covers a broad range of cases, including internet searches, emails, text messages, and phone calls.

Who can use the RIPA?

Only certain organisations are permitted to use the RIPA, and they have different levels of freedom based on how they will use it. For example, the GCHQ (Government Communication Headquarters) and MI6 have the most freedom and require little government approval, while local councils have significantly less.

What are the capabilities of the RIPA?

The RIPA is a very powerful act, and - depending on who's using it - can be used for a wide variety of tasks. Its primary purpose is the tracking of criminals, and to do this it allows investigation, surveillance, and interception.

Investigation allows organisations to view your internet searches, emails, text messages, phone calls, and more. It isn't limited to a certain time frame either, as messages from years previous can be viewed.

Surveillance allows organisations to actively monitor you, be it through your online presence, or physical. They can conduct wiretaps to listen in on phone calls, and plant secret listening devices for real life conversations.

What are the issues with the RIPA?

Due to the nature of the RIPA, it's obviously very controversial. One of these controversies stems from the types of organisations that have access to the RIPA, as they not only include high-profile organisations, but smaller ones like local councils. Some claim that they have no need for direct access to the RIPA, and wouldn't be using it under its defined purposes.

This is supported by the case where a family was being monitored by a local council to check if they were within the catchment area for a local school - something the RIPA is definitely not intended for.

However, many say that these are outweighed by the positives of the RIPA, having solved multiple large cases in the past including the Soham murders.

Another extremely controversial part of the RIPA is that it prevents the existence of such interception activities being revealed in court, meaning that they are never required to prove that it was used in an investigation, or that it was used lawfully.
